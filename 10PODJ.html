The 10 principles of Disability Justice

I. Intersectionality

“We do not live single issue lives” –Audre Lorde. Ableism, coupled with white supremacy, supported by capitalism, underscored by heteropatriarchy, has rendered the vast majority of the world “invalid.” (Sins Invalid, 2015). This principle employs Kimberle Crenshaw’s Intersectionality to critically analyze the experiences of people with disabilities. In other words they use this to explain the intersecting oppression they face for being disabled along with their other marginalized identities such as race, class, sexuality, age etc.

II. Leadership of the Most Impacted

“We are led by those who most know these systems.” –Aurora Levins Morales” (Sins Invalid, 2015). They are elevating, listening to, reading, following, and highlighting the perspectives of those who are most impacted by the systems they fight against when they discuss ableism, racism, sexism and transmisogyny, colonization, police violence, etc. They are not relying on academics and experts to tell them what's what. By emphasizing the leadership of those who will be most affected, they remain grounded in current issues and develop innovative methods of resistance.

III. Anti-Capitalist Politic

In an economy that sees land and humans as components of profit, we are anti-capitalist by the nature of having non-conforming body/minds. (Sins Invalid, 2015). Due to the nature of their physical and mental impairments, they are unable to achieve "normative" levels of productivity in a capitalist society, and as a result, their labor is frequently overlooked by a system that defines it in terms of able-bodied, white supremacist, and gender normative standards. Their value is independent of what they can produce and how much of it.

IV. Commitment to Cross Movement

Shifting how social justice movements understand disability and contextualize ableism, disability justice lends itself to politics of alliance. (Sins Invalid, 2015). Disability justice must collaborate with movements for racial justice, reproductive justice, queer and trans liberation, prison abolition, environmental justice, anti-police terror, Deaf activism, fat liberation, and other forms of justice and liberation in order to reach its full potential as a movement. This calls for addressing ableism-related racism in other movements as well as in communities of white people with disabilities. They develop a cohesive front by working together across several movements.

V. Recognising Wholeness

People have inherent worth outside of commodity relations and capitalist notions of productivity. Each person is full of history and life experience. (Sins Invalid, 2015). Everybody has a rich history and a rich life. Every individual has an interior experience made up of their unique thoughts, feelings, emotions, sexual desires, perceptions, and eccentricities. The disabled are complete human beings.

VI. Sustainability

We learn to pace ourselves, individually and collectively, to be sustained long-term. We value the teachings of our bodies and experiences, and use them as a critical guide and reference point. We take a ground-up approach in identifying the needs of the disability community on our campus. We move away from the urgency of quotas, and our “progress” is marked by the ongoing development of our community-building efforts. Our student leadership makes every effort to enact principles of patience, flexibility, and care, that honors the ways that our capacities may fluctuate over time.

VII. Commitment to cross disability solidarity

Shifting how social justice movements understand disability and contextualize ableism, disability justice lends itself to politics of alliance. Through cross-movement solidarity, we create a united front. We hope that by implementing our Racial Justice Outreach Coordinator and Gender and Sexual Equity Outreach Coordinator, we can facilitate collaborative efforts, and build lasting coalitions with other communities.

VIII. Interdependence

We meet each others’ needs as we build toward liberation, knowing that state solutions inevitably extend into further control over our lives. For the liberation of our community, we offer care and support for each other. In this vein, our Commission works to organize the Disability Community Care Space, a community space for disabled students to talk openly about their experiences and connect with each other.

IX. Collective Access

We move together as people with mixed abilities, multiracial, multi-gendered, mixed class, across the sexual spectrum, with a vision that leaves no bodymind behind.

X. Collective Liberation

No body or mind can be left behind — only by moving together can we accomplish the revolution we require.
